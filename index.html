Irving Rouse's Four Migrations Model: Caribbean Colonization

Irving Rouse proposed four major waves of migration into the Caribbean, each bringing distinct cultural practices, technologies, and ways of life. This model has been both foundational and contested in Caribbean archaeology.

Migration I: Lithic Age

ca. 4000–3000 B.C.
First indigenous explorers from Yucatán Peninsula and Orinoco lowlands arrive in the Caribbean. These peoples brought stone tool technologies (hence "Lithic") and were primarily hunter-gatherers and fishers. Evidence includes stone implements without pottery.

Note: Paleoenvironmental data (charcoal evidence) suggests human presence as early as 5600+ B.C., predating traditional archaeological artifacts by over 1,500 years.

Migration II: Archaic Age

ca. 3000–1000 B.C.
Archaic peoples expand throughout the Caribbean islands, developing more sophisticated maritime technologies and resource exploitation strategies. They modified landscapes through deliberate burning and established seasonal or permanent settlements.

Sites: Banwari Trace (Trinidad), Strombus line (Barbuda), Birgits (Antigua), Rooi Rincon (Curaçao), and others throughout the Lesser and Greater Antilles.

Migration III: Ceramic Age (Saladoid)

ca. 200 B.C.–1500 A.D.
Saladoid agro-ceramists migrate from South America into the Lesser Antilles and Puerto Rico, bringing pottery technology, horticulture (manioc/cassava cultivation), and more sedentary village life. These are the direct ancestors of the Taíno peoples.

The Ceramic Age marks a major cultural shift with elaborate painted pottery, agricultural intensification, and complex social organization. Saladoid peoples are Arawakan-speaking ancestors of later Caribbean nations.

Migration IV: Historic Age

A.D. 1492+
European Invasion & Indigenous Resistance

Columbus's arrival in 1492 initiated violent colonization, enslavement, disease, and genocide of Indigenous Caribbean peoples including Taíno, Kalinago (Carib), Lucayan, and others.

Hood Justice Note: Mainstream narratives claim Taíno "disappeared" by 1584, but this erases living Indigenous descendants and modern identity reclamation movements. Archaeological endpoints ≠ cultural extinction.

Data Conflicts & Debates

First Arrival: Conservative (14,500 B.C.) vs. Expansive (80,000+ B.C.) estimates reflect ongoing debate and Indigenous oral tradition claims.
Caribbean Colonization: Traditional archaeology (Rouse 4000 B.C.) vs. paleoenvironmental data (Siegel 5600+ B.C.) – charcoal shows earlier presence than artifacts.
Saladoid Origins: One migration (Rouse) vs. two/three migrations (Chanlatte, Haviser) – radiocarbon dates don't clearly resolve this.
"Extinction" narrative: A.D. 1584 marks Rouse's archaeological endpoint for Taíno, but living descendants and identity movements continue today.
